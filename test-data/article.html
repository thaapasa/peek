The Quiet Power of Boring Tools

Published 14 March 2025 · 8 minute read

Introduction

There is a particular kind of pleasure that comes from using a tool that has been around long enough to have all of its sharp edges sanded down. It does not surprise you. It does not change its keyboard shortcuts in the next minor release. It does not, in a fit of marketing-driven enthusiasm, decide that what it really wants to be when it grows up is a collaborative cloud platform with a chat sidebar and an AI assistant that suggests completions you did not ask for. It just works, in the unglamorous, undemonstrative way that good infrastructure tends to work.

We do not talk enough about this kind of software. The trade press writes about novelty, because novelty generates clicks, and conference talks reward speakers who can stand on stage and announce a paradigm shift. But most of the work of running a real system, year after year, is done by tools that are old, predictable, and so deeply unfashionable that nobody bothers to argue about them anymore. They are the boring middle of the normal distribution, and they are doing the heavy lifting while the long tail of glittering frameworks fights for attention on the conference circuit.

What “boring” actually buys you

When a tool has been in production at thousands of organisations for fifteen years, a large amount of empirical knowledge has accumulated around it. The failure modes are documented. The performance characteristics are predictable. The interactions with other components — the operating system, the network stack, the file system — have been mapped, mostly the hard way, by someone whose pager went off at three in the morning and who later wrote about the experience on a personal blog that is still, for now, indexed by search engines. This collective experience is a form of capital, and it is not free. It took years to build up, and it is destroyed every time the industry decides to throw out a working tool in favour of a slightly shinier one.

Boring tools also tend to have boring interfaces, by which I mean interfaces that have not changed much in a long time. Muscle memory is real, and it compounds. A senior operator who has used the same shell, the same editor, and the same debugger for a decade is not slower than a beginner using the latest hot tooling; they are substantially faster, and they make fewer mistakes, because they no longer have to think about the tool itself. The tool has become invisible, in the way that a hammer becomes invisible to a carpenter who has used the same one for years.

The cost of constant churn

The opposite of boring is not exciting; it is exhausting. A team that adopts every promising new framework as it appears spends a substantial fraction of its capacity on migrations, rewrites, and the slow archaeology of figuring out what the previous team meant when they reached for an abstraction that has since fallen out of fashion. This is not a hypothetical cost. It shows up in delivery timelines, in employee burnout, and in the strange phenomenon of feature regressions that arrive bundled with a major version bump that was sold as “internal improvements only.”

None of this is an argument against new tools. New tools are how the field improves. But there is an asymmetry between the visible benefits of adopting something new (which are immediate, demonstrable, and easy to put on a slide) and the invisible costs (which are diffuse, long-term, and almost impossible to attribute to any single decision). The asymmetry favours adoption, which is why so many engineering organisations look, in retrospect, like a museum of half-finished migrations.

A modest proposal

The modest proposal is this: when you are choosing a tool for a job that you expect to still exist in five years, weight stability and longevity at least as heavily as feature breadth or developer experience polish. Ask whether the project has a clear maintainer, a deliberate release cadence, and a track record of treating its users like adults who can be trusted with stable interfaces. Ask whether the documentation was written for people doing real work, or for people evaluating the tool over a weekend. Ask, finally, whether you would be embarrassed to still be using this thing in 2035, and if the answer is no, that is probably a sign that you have found something worth keeping.
